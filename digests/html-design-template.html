Macro Trends

The data center energy confrontation reaches a turning point. After months of rising electricity bills and community opposition, the Trump administration's "Bring Your Own Generation" mandate represents a fundamental shift in how Big Tech's power needs will be met. The policy—requiring large data centers to fund dedicated generation rather than drawing from the shared grid—sent nuclear and IPP stocks tumbling, while potentially reshaping the economics of AI infrastructure buildout. Microsoft's "Community-First" pledge suggests tech companies are now competing on community relations, not just compute power.

Permitting reform has fractured along energy source lines. The administration's emergency procedures create a stark two-track system: 14-28 day reviews for fossil fuel projects versus continued delays for renewables. Meanwhile, offshore wind developers have won three consecutive court victories against stop-work orders, but the legal battles are costing millions daily and threatening project economics. The bipartisan SPEED Act's uncertain Senate path suggests comprehensive permitting reform may give way to piecemeal executive action.

The nuclear renaissance gains corporate momentum even as policy uncertainty grows. Meta's 6.6 GW portfolio announcement—spanning existing plants, uprates, and advanced reactors—marks the largest single corporate nuclear commitment in history. Combined with Holtec's first SMR construction permit application and multiple state task forces forming, the industry is building real momentum. Yet the administration's PJM intervention and technology-neutral credit phase-out create crosswinds that may slow deployment timelines even as demand accelerates.
