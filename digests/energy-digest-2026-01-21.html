Macro Trends

Data center demand reshaping grid economics. The PJM capacity auction results starkly illustrate how AI-driven data center growth is transforming electricity markets: data centers now account for nearly half of all capacity costs in the nation's largest grid. This is driving unprecedented utility investment—$1.4 trillion planned by 2030—while simultaneously forcing grid operators like ERCOT and PJM to develop new interconnection processes. Corporate buyers like Meta are responding with landmark nuclear agreements, signaling that clean firm power is becoming essential to meeting 24/7 load requirements.

Transmission emerging as critical bottleneck. The New England Clean Energy Connect's decade-long journey from conception to operation exemplifies both the promise and peril of major transmission projects. As RMI notes, utilities are cutting planned wind and solar additions in half due to changing resource adequacy rules and interconnection delays. Meanwhile, natural gas pipeline capacity is seeing its biggest expansion since 2008—highlighting how permitting reform's benefits may flow unevenly across energy sources. The SPEED Act's House passage and CEQ's NEPA rescission signal continued federal focus on permitting, but Senate action remains uncertain.

Nuclear renaissance gains corporate backing. Meta's agreements with Vistra, TerraPower, and Oklo—combined with DOE's $2.7 billion uranium enrichment investment—mark a inflection point for nuclear energy. Corporate demand is now driving both existing plant life extensions and advanced reactor development, with potential orderbooks reaching 19 advanced reactors backed by private investment. However, the gap between announcement and operation remains significant: most new capacity won't arrive until the 2030s, leaving near-term reliability concerns unaddressed.
